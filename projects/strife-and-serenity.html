01 • Framing the slice

Overview

Strife and Serenity began as my Undergraduate Final Major Project, originally titled Seeds of Conflict. I built it as a solo four month project that earned me a First Class Honours degree, and centred it on Ka'Sipho, a stubborn and emotionally guarded fighter who is pushed into action when one of the few things he cares about is threatened.

I started the project intending to finish it and move on. During the ideation phase I became attached to the world, its characters, and the questions it raised, and the work shifted from a requirement to something I want to continue building towards a full release.

What this slice is trying to prove

  • Combat reads at a glance: enemies broadcast intent clearly, so players feel clever for reacting, not lucky for guessing.
  • Traversal supports emotion: small movement beats create a rhythm of breathe → push → breathe instead of endless arenas.
  • Story is carried through play: ability unlocks, enemy behaviors, and small environmental details all echo the theme of conflict vs calm.

My role

I owned the prologue end-to-end: combat kit design, encounter layouts, narrative beats, moment-to-moment pacing, and implementation in Unity / Game Creator 2.

Earlier in the semester I did a long ideation pass on what I wanted this project to say about conflict, player motivation, and “earned peace”. That thinking shaped every decision in this slice (ideation notes).

North Star:
Combat should feel like a conversation, not a checklist. Every tell, dodge, parry, and counter is a line of dialogue between the player and the enemy — and the story of the prologue is written in those exchanges.
“If players understand the character through how they fight, I’ve done my job.” — Design goal for the prologue

02 • How it plays

Core Loop (Prologue)

The loop had to be readable enough for first-time players, but layered enough to hint at where the full game will go. I framed it as a cycle of Read → Decide → Act → Reflect.

01 • Read
Skirmish setups

Small groups of enemies at clear entry angles teach players how telegraphs, ranges, and Strife/Serenity abilities behave — without dumping a tutorial wall of text.

02 • Decide
Tool choice in the moment

Each encounter is framed so players can ask: “Do I push?” (Strife) or “Do I wait and punish?” (Serenity) — both are valid, but have different risks.

03 • Act
Set-piece arenas & duels

The loop peaks with a contained arena or a duel where the player must combine what they’ve learned: spacing, windows, and emotional stakes from the narrative beats.

04 • Reflect
Traversal & quiet beats

Short traversal segments and small interactions (graffiti, echoes of conflict, idle barks) give players breathing room to mentally file what just happened.

Structuring the prologue like this forced me to be honest about pacing: if a fight didn’t teach something new or pay off a previous beat, it got cut or re-framed.

03 • The toolkit

Key Systems

Mechanically, the prologue is built around a small set of systems tuned to support that “combat as conversation” idea.

Ability Kit

Strife & Serenity styles

Strife favors forward-leaning, high-commitment attacks with strong payoff when the player reads correctly. Serenity leans into timing, counters, and repositioning. Swapping between them lets players decide how they want to express the character in each situation.

Readability

Telegraphs, not surprises

Enemy swings are color and silhouette-coded, with consistent wind-up timings and subtle camera emphasis on big moments. If a player gets hit, the goal is for them to know why instantly — not dig through UI.

Pacing

Traversal as emotional reset

Between heavier fights, players cross short spaces that reframe the stakes: broken shields on the ground, overheard chatter, shifts in lighting temperature. It keeps the prologue from becoming a flat combat montage.

Fail-Forward

Recoverable mistakes

Instead of hard-reset punishments, the slice uses quick recovery states, readable stagger, and generous i-frames on key moves so players can bounce back, learn, and stay in the fantasy even when they misread an attack.

04 • How I built it

Process

Because this was a solo slice, I treated it like a small production: clear pillars, aggressive scoping, and a loop of test → change one thing → test again.

From pillars to play
  • Defined three core pillars: clarity (players always know what’s happening), consequence (their choices matter), and cadence (the rhythm between quiet and loud moments).
  • Sketched paper encounters first, mapping where each pillar shows up: “this fight is about learning to read, this one is about committing, this one is about recovery.”
  • Blocked out levels in Unity, then layered VFX, audio, and narrative beats only once the movement and timings felt right.
Playtests & iteration
  • Ran quick hallway tests to see how fast players picked up tells and counters, then adjusted timings and hitstop.
  • Collected “first-death” stories: asking playtesters to describe what they thought happened helped me spot where communication was breaking.
  • Used a “one friction per pass” rule — each iteration cycle targeted a single issue (camera, readability, stamina pacing) so changes stayed trackable.
  • Discovery & Ideation
    Months of writing, worldbuilding, and questions about conflict, peace, and consequence. The prologue is where those ideas had to survive contact with a controller.
  • Whitebox & Pillar Tests
    Greyboxing arenas and traversal routes to validate pacing before committing to art or polish.
  • Combat & Kit Tuning
    Built and tuned the Strife/Serenity kit, enemy archetypes, and duel behaviours directly against the pillars.
  • Narrative Pass & Polish
    Layered in cutscenes, VO beats, environmental details, and sound to reinforce character and theme without interrupting play.

06 • Looking forward

Results & Takeaways

What landed well

  • Playtesters quickly understood the difference between Strife and Serenity and began swapping styles based on enemy type and mood.
  • Telegraph passes and counter feedback made successful reads feel deliberate rather than lucky, especially in the duel.
  • Traversal and quiet beats between fights gave players space to breathe and made the emotional spikes in the prologue hit harder.

What I’d push further next

  • Introduce one more enemy archetype that pressures Serenity players without overwhelming new players who lean Strife-heavy.
  • Deepen the duel’s reactive behaviour so it mirrors the player’s aggression/passivity more explicitly.
  • Tighten onboarding around advanced tech (perfect parries, stance swaps mid-string) to make those tools feel discoverable rather than “hidden tech”.

Overall, this prologue proved that the Strife & Serenity kit works as a vehicle for story, and that the core pillars — clarity, consequence, and cadence — hold up under real players. The next phase is scaling those ideas across a fuller chapter without losing that readability.
