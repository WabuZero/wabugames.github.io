01 • Framing the slice

Overview

As exciting as it can be, Strife & Serenity isn’t a go-go-go game. It’s a story that you can interact with through its protagonist, Ka’Sipho — shrouded in mystery, where you start off with only a handful of context and go on from there. This prologue is a contained slice that lets players feel that: a taste of what to expect before turning the first page.

What this slice prioritizes

Combat

Establishing the feel of Ka’Sipho’s kit — abilities (Spells, Air Slash, etc) and mechanics (Parrying, Dodging, etc) — and making them work together as a cohesive toolset.

Exploration

Opportunities to gain context by exploring settings, inspired by Xehanort’s Reports (Kingdom Hearts: Birth by Sleep).

Story

Story first and game second — like a book or film you can inhabit, with gameplay meant to elevate immersion rather than interrupt it.

My role

As this was a solo project, I owned it end-to-end: Story/Narrative Design, Abilities and Combat Design, Audio Cues, Lighting, VFX, Pacing and more.

Earlier in the semester I went through a months-long ideation phase, where I explored what I wanted this project to say about conflict, player motivation, and the dichotomy between what someone was trained to be/born to do versus what the world that they live in actually demands. That thinking shaped every decision in this slice (ideation notes).

North Star:
Combat should feel satisfying, like a conversation — where every action is a line of dialogue between the player and the enemy.

02 • How it plays

Core Loop (Prologue)

The loop had to be readable enough for first-time players, but layered enough to hint at where the full game will go.

01 • Arrival
Disorientation

In medias res: you enter the world with limited context, in the middle of a situation. Sparse dialogue, incomplete information, and environmental unease establish that something matters — even if you don’t know what yet. This mirrors Ka’Sipho, who is just as in the dark as the player.

02 • Observe
Exploration as understanding

With only a handful of information, players are encouraged to explore wherever they’re able. Environmental storytelling, optional discoveries (reports, letters, etc), and spatial storytelling provide context without relying on exposition.

03 • Engage
Intentional response

Engagement is defined by how the player chooses to act: combat when conflict is unavoidable, traversal (Jump, Dodge, Sprint, Grapple) as expression, and restraint as a meaningful choice. Traversal is authored to communicate Ka’Sipho’s physicality and caution — not just move the player forward.

04 • Reflect
Quiet beats after intensity

After heavier encounters, short traversal segments and small interactions create breathing room. These beats help the prologue stay reflective, letting players mentally file what just happened before moving on.

Although most of the settings in this prologue have an old and abandoned feel, making them still feel alive was one of the biggest challenges — especially when exploration is meant to do so much of the immersion work.

03 • The toolkit

Key Systems

Mechanically, the prologue is built around a small set of systems tuned to support that “combat as conversation” idea.

Ability Kit

Strife & Serenity styles

Strife favors forward-leaning, high-commitment attacks with strong payoff when the player reads correctly. Serenity leans into timing, counters, and repositioning. Swapping between them lets players decide how they want to express the character in each situation.

Readability

Telegraphs, not surprises

Enemy swings are color and silhouette-coded, with consistent wind-up timings and subtle camera emphasis on big moments. If a player gets hit, the goal is for them to know why instantly — not dig through UI.

Pacing

Traversal as emotional reset

Between heavier fights, players cross short spaces that reframe the stakes: broken shields on the ground, overheard chatter, shifts in lighting temperature. It keeps the prologue from becoming a flat combat montage.

Fail-Forward

Recoverable mistakes

Instead of hard-reset punishments, the slice uses quick recovery states, readable stagger, and generous i-frames on key moves so players can bounce back, learn, and stay in the fantasy even when they misread an attack.

04 • Reward & reinforcement

Feedback Loops

One of the biggest things that Strife & Serenity has to offer is its reinforcement and rewarding of intentional, not passive engagement.

01 • Marks

Enhancement artifacts

Marks are enhancement artifacts that can be attached to the player’s weaponry to modify stats and behaviour. Early iterations explored a Materia-inspired approach (Final Fantasy VII), where slotted Marks would determine which spells or abilities could be cast. While I’d keep the idea of being able to slot abilities or spells to a hotbar to be used in the field, for the prologue, Marks instead function as reinforcements rather than permissions.

They enhance what the player already chooses to do without locking core abilities behind configuration. This keeps the system readable for first-time players while still hinting at deeper customization in the full game. Overall, Marks allow progression to feel more personal.

02 • Adaptive Combat Gauge

Momentum as a temporary reward

Combat is further reinforced through an adaptive gauge that responds to variety and momentum. Using different abilities and spells fills the gauge, which increases Ka’Sipho’s combat level up to a temporary cap (3).

  • Spells & abilities deal increased damage
  • Stronger, more useful status effects on hit
  • Basic attacks become more potent

This heightened state is intentionally temporary. Once it expires, the gauge resets — encouraging players to stay engaged and continue experimenting rather than settling into a single safe pattern.

Early iterations explored a command-gauge–inspired system (Kingdom Hearts: Birth by Sleep), where chaining specific abilities or spells would push combat into element-specific states (for example, repeated ice attacks leading to a frost-aligned state). For scope and clarity, this approach was ultimately simplified into a more linear system.

Why this matters
Together, these systems ensure that progression feels like feedback rather than escalation. The game responds to how the player plays, and growth is felt moment to moment rather than inflated stats or permanent advantages.

From a player’s perspective, this approach reflects a personal appreciation for systems that acknowledge intentional play. When a game rewards players for engaging fully with its mechanics, it creates a quiet dialogue between designer and player.
05 • How I built it

Process

Although Strife & Serenity is a solo slice, its development followed a structured, multi-phase process. Rather than starting with mechanics, the project began with questions — about conflict, balance, identity, and the cost of resolution — and worked outward toward play.

Ideation & thematic foundation

The project began with an extended ideation phase, documented in detail on my Notion page. I explored a wide range of themes — identity, power, balance, determination, redemption — before deliberately narrowing them down. Through research, reflection, and media analysis, conflict emerged as the foundational theme, both narratively and mechanically.

This phase wasn’t about generating a game idea immediately, but about understanding what the game should be about, and what it should ask of the player emotionally. That thematic groundwork shaped every decision that followed.

Story discovery through music collaboration

As the concept matured, I collaborated with music students to develop the tone and emotional language of the world. To support this, I created 14 short concept videos, each focused on a specific moment, mood, or narrative beat.

  • Rough story context
  • Visual references and pacing
  • Inspiration tracks closest to how I imagined the final soundtrack

What began as music reference material evolved into proto-storyboards, helping refine narrative flow, solidify key moments, and lock the emotional arc of the prologue before major implementation began. (concept video playlist)

Mechanical prototyping & visual exploration

With the story established, I transitioned into Unity to explore how the game should feel. I focused first on movement, abilities, and spells — testing how traversal and combat could express character and theme rather than just function. Particular attention was given to spell identity and status effects, ensuring each ability felt distinct and readable while supporting the adaptive combat philosophy.

Mechanics were iterated visually and in motion early, allowing feel and clarity to drive design decisions before committing to polish.

Worldbuilding, art & iteration

Once the core mechanics and narrative beats were in place, I moved into environment design and worldbuilding. Settings were designed to feel aged, abandoned, but not lifeless — spaces that suggest history rather than explain it outright. I collaborated with art students on key art and cutscene elements, ensuring visual storytelling aligned with the tone established earlier.

Throughout development, the project followed a tight iteration loop: test → adjust one thing → test again. This kept scope controlled while allowing the slice to steadily converge toward its intended experience.

Outcome
By the end of the process, the prologue emerged as a focused, intentional slice — not a feature showcase, but a tonal and thematic introduction to Strife & Serenity. Each phase informed the next, allowing story, sound, mechanics, and visuals to develop in conversation with one another rather than in isolation.

06 • Looking forward

Results & Takeaways

What landed well

  • Playtesters quickly understood the difference between Strife and Serenity and began swapping styles based on enemy type and mood.
  • Telegraph passes and counter feedback made successful reads feel deliberate rather than lucky, especially in the duel.
  • Traversal and quiet beats between fights gave players space to breathe and made the emotional spikes in the prologue hit harder.

What I’d push further next

  • Introduce one more enemy archetype that pressures Serenity players without overwhelming new players who lean Strife-heavy.
  • Deepen the duel’s reactive behaviour so it mirrors the player’s aggression/passivity more explicitly.
  • Tighten onboarding around advanced tech (perfect parries, stance swaps mid-string) to make those tools feel discoverable rather than “hidden tech”.

Overall, this prologue proved that the Strife & Serenity kit works as a vehicle for story, and that the core pillars — clarity, consequence, and cadence — hold up under real players. The next phase is scaling those ideas across a fuller chapter without losing that readability.
