01 • Framing the slice

Overview

As exciting as it can be, Strife & Serenity isn’t built to be constant adrenaline. It’s a playable mystery: you begin in medias res with incomplete context, and your understanding grows through movement, atmosphere, optional discoveries, and the way Ka’Sipho fights.

Priority
Story-First
Gameplay exists to elevate narrative tone—like a book you can inhabit.
Priority
Exploration
Optional discoveries (reports/letters) that deepen context without halting pacing.
Priority
Combat Kit
Abilities, mechanics (parry/dodge), and “feel” stitched into one readable kit.

What this prologue is trying to prove

  • Readable tension: the player senses that “something is wrong” before being told.
  • Understanding through discovery: context comes from space + optional artifacts, not exposition dumps.
  • Character through control: Ka’Sipho’s identity shows up in how he moves, hesitates, commits, and recovers.

My role

Solo end-to-end: Story/Narrative Design, Abilities & Combat Design, Audio cues, Lighting, VFX, Pacing, and implementation.

North Star:
Combat should feel satisfying—like a conversation. Every action is a line of dialogue between the player and the enemy.
“Throw the player into the dark—then let them earn the light.” — Prologue goal (mystery + discovery)
Deep dive Ideation foundation

Earlier in the semester I went through a months-long ideation phase exploring conflict, player motivation, and the tension between what someone was trained to be vs. what their world demands. That thinking shaped every decision in this slice.
02 • How it plays

Core Loop (Prologue)

The loop is designed to be skimmable for first-time players but layered enough to hint at the full game. It’s a cycle of Disorient → Observe → Engage → Reflect.

01 • Arrival
Disorientation (In Medias Res)

You enter with limited context. Sparse dialogue, incomplete information, and environmental unease mirror Ka’Sipho’s own uncertainty.

02 • Observe
Exploration as understanding

The world teaches without speeches: environmental storytelling + optional discoveries (reports, letters, etc.) deepen meaning.

03 • Engage
Intentional response

Engagement isn’t only combat: traversal (jump/dodge/sprint/grapple) and restraint are meaningful choices, not downtime.

04 • Reflect
Breath + consequence

Quiet beats let tension land. The game gives room to process, then asks you to move forward anyway.

What I wanted players to feel emotional goals
＋
  • Curiosity before confidence: “What happened here?” comes before “I’m in control.”
  • Physicality: traversal sells caution and capability, not just speed.
  • Earned momentum: power spikes come from intentional play, not passive stat inflation.
Worldbuilding via artifacts reports + letters
＋
Exploration rewards context. Optional discoveries work like “field reports” — inspired by Kingdom Hearts: Birth By Sleep’s report-style lore delivery — letting players opt into deeper understanding.
03 • The toolkit

Key Systems

This prologue isn’t only “combat systems.” It’s also progression + customization that reinforces intentional play — especially through Marks and the Adaptive Combat Gauge.

Combat Identity

Strife & Serenity styles

Strife leans forward: higher commitment, bigger payoff when you read correctly. Serenity rewards timing, counters, and repositioning. Switching style is expression: “who am I in this moment?”

World & Discovery

Optional context (reports, letters)

Exploration isn’t loot-hunting — it’s narrative clarity. Optional artifacts add meaning without forcing exposition, letting players choose how deep they want to go.

Progression

Marks (enhancement gems)

Marks attach to weaponry to modify stats/behavior. Early iterations explored a Materia-style “spells depend on slots,” but the prologue uses Marks as reinforcement rather than permission — enhancing what the player chooses to do.

Reward Loop

Adaptive Combat Gauge

Variety and momentum fill a gauge. When full, Ka’Sipho’s combat level temporarily rises (cap 3), boosting damage/status impact and encouraging experimentation instead of safe repetition.

Design decision: Marks Materia debate
＋
Early on, I debated making Marks work like FF7 Materia: slot a Mark → gain a spell/ability. I still like the long-term idea of slotting abilities/spells into a field hotbar — but for the prologue, Marks became enhancements that amplify the player’s intent without hiding core abilities behind configuration.
  • Pro: clearer onboarding for first-time players
  • Pro: still hints at deep customization later
  • Tradeoff: less “buildcraft” in the earliest slice
Design decision: Gauge system KH:BBS iteration
＋
I explored a command-gauge-inspired approach (Kingdom Hearts: Birth by Sleep), where specific spell sequences would shift combat into element-aligned states (e.g., frequent ice attacks → frost state). For scope/readability, this was simplified into a more linear system for the prologue — while preserving the core goal: reward variety and momentum.
Why this matters:
Marks + the Gauge make progression feel like feedback, not escalation. The game responds to how you play — creating a quiet designer ↔ player dialogue that rewards intentional engagement.
04 • How I built it

Process

Although this is a solo slice, development followed a structured multi-phase process. It began with questions (conflict, balance, identity, the cost of resolution) and worked outward toward play.

Ideation & thematic foundation

I explored identity, power, balance, determination, redemption — then deliberately narrowed down. Conflict became the foundation both narratively and mechanically. This wasn’t about “finding a game idea fast,” but about defining what the game should ask of the player emotionally.

Story discovery through music collaboration

I collaborated with music students and created 14 concept videos for specific moments/moods. They started as music reference, but evolved into proto-storyboards: story ideas surfaced, scenes connected, and the emotional arc locked in before heavy implementation began.

Mechanical prototyping & visual exploration feel first

With story established, I moved into Unity focusing on movement, abilities, and spells — testing how traversal/combat could express character and theme rather than just function. Clarity and spell identity were iterated early in motion before committing to polish.

06 • Looking forward

Results & Takeaways

What landed well

  • Playtesters quickly understood the difference between Strife and Serenity and began swapping styles based on enemy type and mood.
  • Telegraph passes and counter feedback made successful reads feel deliberate rather than lucky, especially in the duel.
  • Traversal and quiet beats between fights gave players space to breathe and made the emotional spikes in the prologue hit harder.

What I’d push further next

  • Introduce one more enemy archetype that pressures Serenity players without overwhelming new players who lean Strife-heavy.
  • Deepen the duel’s reactive behaviour so it mirrors the player’s aggression/passivity more explicitly.
  • Tighten onboarding around advanced tech (perfect parries, stance swaps mid-string) to make those tools feel discoverable rather than “hidden tech”.

Overall, this prologue proved that the Strife & Serenity kit works as a vehicle for story, and that the core pillars — clarity, consequence, and cadence — hold up under real players. The next phase is scaling those ideas across a fuller chapter without losing that readability.
